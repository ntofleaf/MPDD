Abstract
Video-based automatic depression assessment (ADA)
system have been widely explored and deployed for real-
world usage. While face videos recorded for ADA contains
sensitive and private contents, existing standard depression
face video delivery and storage strategies lack effective pri-
vacy protection mechanism. To address this, this paper pro-
poses the first depression face video steganography strat-
egy which enables the invertible concealment of sensitive
depression face videos, while ensuring user privacy. We
decompose the facial secret video into individual frames
and hide each secret frame into a cover image of the same
size, enabling high-capacity steganography for video data.
Meanwhile, we leverage Stable Diffusion to allow users to
generate their own cover videos, enhancing controllability.
During the hiding process, we propose a novel Invertible
Network to ensure the precise recovery of facial attributes
and behavioral information. Experimental results demon-
strate that our video steganography method largely pre-
serves the accuracy of the assessment of depression from
facial videos. Our code will be provided upon acceptance.
1. Introduction
Depression is a prevalent mental disorder that affects more
than 300 million people worldwide [9].
With the con-
tinuous advancement of machine learning, facial video-
based automatic depression assessment (ADA) methods
[6, 14, 15, 17, 18, 22, 23, 29–31] that infer depression from
human facial behaviour dynamics have been widely ex-
plored. Despite the promising prospects of face video-based
ADA in real-world applications, such face videos typically
contain highly sensitive personal information (e.g., identity,
age and gender) However, existing mainstream mechanisms
for face video transmission and storage offer limited pri-
vacy protections, posing a significant risk of sensitive men-
tal health-related behavioral data leakage. Therefore, there
is an urgent need for more secure depression face video pro-
tection strategy, where face video steganography that allows
sensitive facial information to be embedded into visually
plausible non-face videos and transmitted covertly [34], of-
fers a novel perspective and a practical solution.
In recent years, benchmark datasets have played a cru-
cial role in advancing Automatic Depression Assessment
(ADA) from facial videos. Early datasets such as AVEC
2013 [26], AVEC 2014 [27], VLOG [1], and CMDC [36]
provided raw or lightly processed face videos. While hu-
man facial behaviours contain rich cues in reflecting human
emotional and affect status [3, 11, 12], these videos pose se-
rious privacy risks due to their identity-revealing nature. To
mitigate this, later datasets like AVEC 2016 [28], 2017 [19],
and 2019 [20] avoid releasing raw videos and instead of-
fer extracted facial features such as AUs, head poses, and
expressions.
Although this reduces privacy exposure, it
comes at the cost of losing contextual and emotional in-
formation critical for accurate depression analysis. In in-
dustry, raw patient videos are still commonly recorded for
telehealth or mental health screening, often without strong
privacy-preserving protocols for sharing or storage
[7].
These limitations highlight the urgent need for a secure,
information-preserving video sharing method. Motivated
by this, we propose a novel steganographic framework tai-
lored for ADA, which enables effective analysis while safe-
guarding sensitive facial data.

semantics, enabling flexible and context-aware depression
face video steganography.
In this paper, we propose the pioneering, simple but
effective depression face video steganography framework
(called DepFaceSteg (DFS)) encoding all frames in the
secret face video of an arbitrary length into another un-
relevant non-face video containing the same of more
frames, which can be almost fully re-constructed at the re-
veal stage. In the face video steganography stage, our DFS
starts with automatically generating a customized cover
video that matches the target resolution, length, and de-
sired appearance using a text-conditioned diffusion model,
allowing flexible and user-defined visual control. Then, it
decomposes all face frames from the secret face video, and
involves an invertible network to encode them into the syn-
thesised cover video, allowing high-capacity hiding while
preserving fine-grained facial behaviors crucial for depres-
sion analysis. In the reveal stage, the same invertible net-
work is employed to accurately reconstruct the original se-
cret video frames from the stego video without any auxil-
iary information, preserving not only the pixel-level fidelity
but also subtle facial attributes and behavioral cues essen-
tial for downstream depression assessment tasks. The entire
framework of our approach is illustrated in Fig. 1. The main
contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
• We propose the first depression face video steganography
framework to enhance user privacy protection for face
video-based ADA task, which enables the generation of
customized cover videos for effective face video hiding.
• Experimental results confirms the effectiveness of our pi-
oneering face video steganography approach in securing
user face video data transmission, suggesting that our ap-
proach allows not only effective face video hiding but also
recovering high-quality face video (evidenced by almost
the same ADA results achieved by original and recovered

2.1. Controllable cover video generation
To enhance the imperceptibility, flexibility, and security of
the steganographic process, we extend a diffusion-based
video generation model — AnimateDiff [5]. Unlike fixed
cover videos, which may introduce semantic or structural
correlations that risk exposing hidden content, using gener-
ative diffusion models allows us to synthesize cover videos
that are both visually realistic and semantically irrelevant
to facial data, significantly improving the security of the
hidden transmission. Users can specify the desired con-
tent of the generated video using natural language prompts,
such as ”a sunset beach with waves” or ”a futuristic city in
anime style.” AnimateDiff encodes the prompt and gener-
ates a video sequence that matches the number of frames
and spatial resolution of the target face video to be con-
cealed, serving as Vcov. This generation approach offers
several advantages:
• Controllability: Users have full flexibility to define the
video style and content.
Figure 2. The architecture of our invertible i-Net.
• High Imperceptibility: The generated video is semanti-
cally unrelated to facial data, making it difficult to infer
the hidden content.
• Structural Alignment: Techniques such as motion guid-
ance can be employed to ensure that the generated video
aligns with the secret video in both dimensions and tem-
poral rhythm.
In practice, we utilize the DDIM sampler to accelerate the
generation process, and apply frame alignment to the output
video to ensure consistency with Vsec in terms of resolution
and frame count. which not only preserves the visual real-
ism of the generated cover video but also securely hides the
depression-relevant facial signals, thereby enabling privacy-
preserving video transmission and storage.
2.2. Invertible network for face video steganogra-
phy and recover
The key challenge of depression face video steganogra-
phy lies in embedding and restoring fine-grained facial be-
havioural information (e.g., micro-expressions, gaze, and
posture) for ADA while maintaining high visual quality and
imperceptibility. This is particularly critical in applications
like ADA, where even subtle distortions may compromise
downstream performance.
To address this, we design a
frame-level steganographic module based on invertible net-
work architectures, enabling lossless and high-capacity en-
coding of facial frames into unrelated cover videos. Our
method is built upon on a Invertible Network, which allows
for bijective mappings between the input and output spaces.
This means the same model can be used both for hiding and
revealing, without requiring any side information.
Network architecture: As illustrated in Fig. 2, our i-
Net employs a set of invertible conceal and reveal blocks,
which share the same network parameters but operate in op-
posite information flow directions. The i-th hiding block in
the forward process takes Icov(i) and Isec(i) as the input,
and outputs Icov(i + 1) and Isec(i + 1) as:
Icov(i + 1) = Icov(i) + ϕ(Isec(i)),
Isec(i + 1) = Isec(i) ⊙exp (α(ρ(Icov(i + 1)))) + η(Icov(i + 1))
(3)

where α(·) is a sigmoid function scaled by a constant factor
for clamping purposes, and ⊙denotes element-wise mul-
tiplication. The functions ρ(·), ϕ(·), and η(·) are imple-
mented using dense blocks, which are widely adopted for
their strong representational capacity [4]. After the final
conceal block, we obtain the stego image Isteg along with
the lost information. Here each conceal block applies re-
versible transformations: the update to the cover image is
an additive function, and the secret image transformation
combines element-wise operations (exponentiation and ad-
dition) with functions that can be inverted using the same
network structure in reverse. In addition, sharing param-
eters between forward and reverse passes ensures symme-
try for perfect reconstruction. As a result, our i-Net is in-
vertible, which enables the model to embed rich facial de-
tails into a visually natural frame, thereby preserving the
semantic fidelity essential for downstream ADA task. In
particular, our invertible design not only ensures lossless
reconstruction where facial details—such as expressions,
gaze, and posture—are fully recoverable, preserving both
visual and semantic fidelity, but also minimal perceptual
distortion where the generated stego frames remain indis-
tinguishable from the original cover frames. Consequently,
the standard invertible network training [10] used in this pa-
per jointly optimize the embedding and extraction processes
to maximize hiding capacity while preserving visual simi-
larity between the stego and cover images.

3. Experiment
3.1. Experimental settings
Datasets: We adopt the AVEC 2014 face video depression
assessment dataset to evaluate the effectiveness of our pro-
posed depression face video steganography method. It in-
volves two types of recording conditions: Northwind and
Freeform, each of which records 150 clips ranging from 6
seconds to 4 mins 8 seconds. Both datasets were evenly di-
vided into three subsets: training, development and testing.
Metrics: We adopt two quantitative metrics to evaluate the
quality of both cover/stego video pairs and secret/recovery
video pairs: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) and Struc-
tural Similarity Index (SSIM). PSNR is used to measure
the pixel-level fidelity between corresponding frames, while
SSIM evaluates structural similarity and perceptual qual-
ity.
Higher values of PSNR and SSIM indicate better
visual quality and more faithful reconstruction.
To fur-
ther assess the semantic fidelity of the recovered video
in the context of ADA, we follow [23] and employ a set
of evaluation metrics including Root Mean Square Error
(RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Pearson Correla-
tion Coefficient (PCC), and Concordance Correlation Coef-
ficient (CCC). These metrics jointly capture the regression
accuracy and consistency between predicted and ground-
truth depression scores.
Implementation details: Each video from the AVEC 2014
dataset is used as a secret video input. The secret video
is first decomposed into individual frames, which are then
one-to-one aligned with the frames of a generated cover
video. Each secret frame is embedded into its correspond-
ing cover frame using the reversible steganographic net-
work. The resulting stego frames are then reassembled into
a stego video, and we compute PSNR and SSIM to evaluate
the quality of the hiding process. Next, the stego frames
are passed through the decoding phase of the reversible
network to reveal the recovered secret frames, which are
subsequently reassembled into the recovered secret video.
This recovered video is then fed into the ADA evaluation
pipeline. The affective analysis results are compared with
those obtained from the original AVEC2014 videos to as-
sess the semantic fidelity of the recovered content.

4. Conclusion
This paper proposes the first ADA-oriented face video
steganography framework that leverages a reversible hid-
ing mechanism and controllable cover video generation,
which achieves high-capacity and high-fidelity information
hiding with enhanced imperceptibility and enables flexible,
prompt-driven control over the cover content. Extensive ex-
periments demonstrated that our framework achieves strong
performance in terms of PSNR and SSIM, indicating min-
imal visual distortion in both stego and recovered videos,
validating the semantic integrity of our approach.
